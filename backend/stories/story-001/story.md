# The Lantern in the Hollow

The road had long since given up on being a road. What remained was a suggestion of packed earth winding between birch trees, their bark peeling like old paper. Mara pulled her coat tighter and told herself the cold was only the evening settling in.

She had walked since dawn. The map promised a village by dusk, but dusk had come and gone, taking the last of her certainty with it. When the first light appeared between the trees, small and amber and trembling, she almost wept with relief.

It was a lantern. Hung from a low branch, swaying though there was no wind, it cast a circle of warm gold on the forest floor. Mara stepped toward it. As she did, a second light kindled further on, then a third, a trail of patient flames leading deeper into the hollow.

Reason told her to turn back. But reason had not kept her warm, and reason did not know the way home. She followed.

The lanterns led her to a clearing she could not have imagined. At its center stood a well of pale stone, and around it, figures woven from light sat in a silent circle, as if waiting for her arrival.

One of them rose. It had the shape of an old woman, edges soft as candle smoke. "You followed the lights," it said, and its voice was the sound of pages turning. "Most travelers run."

"I had nowhere else to run to," Mara answered.

The figure considered this, then gestured to the well. "Then look, and decide what you are looking for. The hollow shows each traveler a different road. Some find their way home. Some find a better question."

Mara leaned over the well. The water was perfectly still, and in it she saw not her reflection but a door, ordinary and wooden, with light spilling beneath it. It was the door of a house she had not seen in twelve years.

She understood then that the forest had not lost her. She had lost herself long before the road ran out, and the lanterns had simply been honest enough to show her so.

When morning came, the clearing was empty, the lanterns gone, the well no more than a ring of weathered stone. But there was a path now, clear and sunlit, and Mara walked it without once looking back.
